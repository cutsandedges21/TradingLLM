# Valuation & Fundamentals

Technical analysis asks "where is price going?" **Fundamental** analysis asks "what is this business worth?" Over years, price tends to follow value; over days, it follows mood.

**The headline metric — P/E ratio** (price ÷ earnings per share):
- It's how many dollars you pay for $1 of annual profit. A P/E of 20 ≈ paying $20 per $1 of earnings.
- **High P/E** = the market expects strong future growth (or it's overhyped). **Low P/E** = modest expectations (or a value bargain... or a struggling business — a "value trap").
- P/E only means something *relative* — vs the company's history, its peers, and its growth rate.

**Other building blocks:**
- **EPS** (earnings per share) and its *growth* — is the business making more money over time?
- **Revenue growth**, **margins** (how much of each sale becomes profit), and **debt** (leverage cuts both ways).
- **Dividend yield**: cash paid to shareholders ÷ price — income, and a signal of maturity.

**Growth vs Value** are two camps: *growth* investors pay up for fast-expanding companies; *value* investors hunt for solid businesses priced cheaply. Neither is "right" — they win in different regimes.

**The honest limit:** a great company can be a terrible stock if you overpay, and a mediocre one can be a great trade if it's cheap enough. *Price is what you pay; value is what you get.*
