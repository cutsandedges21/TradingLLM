# How Markets Actually Work

A stock price isn't one number — it's two. The **bid** is the highest price buyers will pay; the **ask** (or offer) is the lowest price sellers will accept. The gap between them is the **spread**.

- **You buy at the ask, sell at the bid.** The spread is a hidden cost you pay on every round trip.
- **Liquidity** = how easily you can trade without moving the price. Big names (AAPL, SPY) are very liquid — tight spreads, huge volume. Tiny stocks and some crypto are *illiquid* — wide spreads, and your own order can move the price against you.
- **Volume** is how many shares traded. High volume = lots of participants = more reliable price signals.

When you submit an order, it goes to an exchange and is matched against resting orders in the **order book**. A liquid market fills you almost instantly near the quoted price; a thin one may fill you worse than you expected — that gap is **slippage**.

**Why it matters for you:** every trade has friction (spread + slippage + any fees). Frequent trading in illiquid names quietly bleeds returns. This app's paper broker even models a small cost so backtests stay honest.

> Rule of thumb: prefer liquid instruments while learning. Tight spreads and deep volume are a beginner's best friend.
