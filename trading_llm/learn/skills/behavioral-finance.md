# Trading Psychology & Biases

Your biggest opponent isn't the market — it's your own brain, which evolved for survival, not for buying low and selling high. Naming the traps is the first defense.

- **Disposition effect**: selling winners too early (to "lock in" a small gain) and holding losers too long (to "avoid" realizing a loss). It's exactly backwards — it cuts your wins short and lets your losses grow.
- **Overtrading**: trading out of boredom or to "make something happen." Each trade costs spread + slippage; activity feels productive but usually erodes returns. Doing nothing is a position.
- **FOMO** (fear of missing out): chasing a stock that already ran because you can't stand watching it without you. You buy the top, it reverses, you panic-sell the bottom.
- **Anchoring**: fixating on a number — your entry price, an old high — and refusing to act until price "gets back" there. The market doesn't know or care about your cost basis.
- **Confirmation bias**: only reading news that agrees with your position. Actively seek the *bear* case for your *bull* trade.
- **Recency bias**: assuming the recent trend (up or down) will continue forever, right before it doesn't.

**The fix is process, not willpower:** decide rules in advance (entry, stop, size), write down *why* you took each trade, and review honestly. A journal turns vague feelings into visible patterns you can correct.
